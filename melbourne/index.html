          The mood, day by day

          Each evening's discussion scored and added to the line. The story is in the drift — weekend lifts, wet-week dips, the odd spike when something kicks off.

          How it's built

          scrape→ sentiment→ summarise→ commit JSON→ static render

          A small Python job runs on my machine each morning. It pulls the day's most-engaged #melbourne posts from the fediverse (Mastodon's public tag timelines via aus.social), the day's weather and air quality (Open-Meteo), what's on (Ticketmaster) and a handful of local headlines (RSS). Every post is scored with VADER for sentiment; Claude writes the short digest. The job commits two JSON files into this site and pushes — so the page you're reading is plain static HTML with no backend and nothing called at view time.

          Why the fediverse and not Reddit? Reddit now blocks unauthenticated reads outright (a network-level block, even from a browser), so the open, keyless Mastodon timelines are both the practical choice and the more honest one. The trend is the point of the piece: one snapshot is just a mood ring; a line over weeks is where patterns show up, so each run appends a row to a history file.

          Where it's honest about itself

          • VADER misses tone. It's a lexicon model — fast and transparent, but blind to sarcasm and local slang (a post calling Metro delays "great, just great" reads as positive). Treat the score as a coarse weather-vane, not a verdict.
          • Small daily samples. A quiet day might be 20-30 posts; the mean can swing on a single popular post. Sentiment on the fediverse also runs gentler than Reddit — fewer pile-ons — so expect a kinder baseline.
          • The fediverse isn't a city. #melbourne on Mastodon skews techie, arty and older than Melbourne at large. It's a lens, not a census.
          • Thin at launch. A tag timeline is only ever "recent", so sentiment history can't be backfilled — the line starts short and fills in daily. Weather history seeds the overlay so the chart isn't a lone dot on day one.

          A daily, self-refreshing read on the city — and an honest one about how far a lexicon model and one subreddit can actually see.
