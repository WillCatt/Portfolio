Machine Learning in Football · 02

Every match, predicted — then marked

A Dixon-Coles goals model turns 150 years of international results into a probability for every scoreline of every World Cup match. The catch is accountability: the model is frozen the day before kickoff, so no result can leak back into it, and then it's graded against what actually happens. See how it rates any match — then watch it mark its own homework as the tournament unfolds.

Part I

How the model sees a match

Pick a fixture

Win / draw / loss probabilities, expected goals, the most likely scorelines, and the full score grid — the Dixon-Coles model's complete read on the game. Host nations get a venue edge only when they play in their own country.

Even a 60% favourite loses two times in five. A forecast isn't a prediction of what will happen — it's an honest spread of what might.

01

What the model believes

Every team in two numbers

The model reduces 150 years of results to just two ratings per team — how much it tends to score (attack) and how much it concedes (defence). That is its entire opinion of the world. Up and to the right is elite at both ends; the dashed lines mark the field average.

Strengths are log-scale coefficients from the Dixon-Coles fit at a 2-year half-life. Hover any dot for the team. Two numbers are all that separate a favourite from an underdog here.

03

What makes a rating move

How much should the recent past count?

The fit weights older matches less, on a half-life you can choose. Weight all history equally and old dynasties look strong forever; weight only the last few months and a team in form leaps up. These are the teams the choice moves most.

Overall rating (attack + defence) under each half-life, from all-history on the left to a 6-month window on the right. The published model uses the 2-year setting — a deliberate middle ground.

04

Does the money agree?

On-pitch strength vs squad value

A reality check on the results-only ratings: how well does each squad's Transfermarkt market value line up with what the model learned from matches? Closely — but not perfectly, which is exactly why squad value is offered as an optional extra, not a core term.

Each team's market-value z-score against its fitted overall rating. The model blends value in only when you ask it to (see the dials below), and even then at a gentle 0.15 weight.

A model is a stack of assumptions. The honest move isn't to hide them — it's to hand you the dials.

One formula, fully exposed

The whole model is four ideas — attack, defence, a fitted home edge and a low-score draw tweak. No hidden layers; every number on this page comes straight out of that formula.

Ratings, not rankings

Each team is two continuous numbers learned from results — not a seeding or a bracket. That's what lets the model price any matchup, including ones that have never been played.

Choices you can see

How far back history counts, and whether money matters, are judgement calls. The honest move is to show the dials and let you watch the odds move — not to bury them.
