Machine Learning in Football · 06

A coin flip that isn't

A penalty looks like luck and feels like nerve. Read enough of them as geometry and it becomes something else: the corners win, dead centre is wasted, keepers are guessing in the dark — and the data flatly refuses to tell you which way they dive. Four acts on where the ball goes, then take the gloves yourself.

Act II

Where keepers go

You can't actually measure where keepers dive

The dirty secret of penalty analytics on open data: there is no dive-direction column. You only know which way a keeper went when they saved it. A keeper beaten by a perfect strike is recorded identically to one who guessed wrong — a clean goal, direction unknown.

What we can measure (all shot placements) vs the sliver of dives we can actually see — recoverable for only — of kicks, biased toward shots hit near the keeper. Anyone quoting a tidy “keepers dive right 57%” from data like this is filling the gap with a guess.

The most-cited keeper statistic in football — which way they dive — is the one this data refuses to give up.

Act III

Is anyone playing Nash?

If every placement scored equally, shooters would be at equilibrium

Game theory's one clean prediction: at a mixed-strategy equilibrium, every option you use must pay the same. For a taker, a zone's payoff is just how often it scores. So — do the zones shooters use convert equally? (Chiappori, Groseclose & Levitt 2002; Palacios-Huerta 2003.)

The payoff

You're the keeper

Five kicks. Real placements. No coin flips.

Each kick is a real StatsBomb penalty, drawn from the distribution you just saw. Pick your dive before the ball is struck. Guess the side and you still have to reach it — a perfect kick into the top corner beats you anyway.

Act IV

The pressure cooker

Is the fifth kick where shootouts are won — or just where the best takers wait?

Conversion by the taker's order number, with 95% CIs. The bars are wide — there simply aren't many fifth kicks in open data.

The confound: kick order is manager-chosen, so order effects and taker quality are entangled. Even a real dip at kick five wouldn't prove pressure — it could just be who managers choose to send when.

The fifth kick looks no scarier than the first — once you remember that managers, not chance, decide who takes it.
